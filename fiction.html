Giver

Brave New World

Brave New World

Author:
Aldous Huxley.
Published:
1932.
Genre:
Dystopian, science fiction, social commentary.
Setting:
London and a "Savage Reservation," in the World State, circa the year 2540 AD (or AF 632, "After Ford").

Set in a futuristic world ruled by a powerful global government called the World State, society is built on the principles of stability, uniformity, and pleasure. Natural birth, families, and individuality have been eliminated. Instead, humans are genetically engineered and conditioned from birth into strict social castes (Alpha to Epsilon) designed for specific roles. People live lives filled with instant gratification, encouraged to consume endlessly and avoid discomfort. To suppress negative emotions, they use a drug called soma, which ensures people remain content and unquestioning. The story follows Bernard Marx, an Alpha who feels alienated and dissatisfied with the shallow pleasures of society. He travels to a Savage Reservation with Lenina Crowne, a conformist Beta. There, they meet John, a man born naturally and raised outside the World State, with traditional human values and knowledge of Shakespeare. When Bernard brings John back to the World State, he becomes a sensation — the “Savage” everyone wants to see. But John is horrified by the lack of meaning, love, and individuality in this seemingly perfect society. He struggles to find a place in this world, ultimately leading to a tragic end.

City Of Embers
Author:
Jeanne DuPrau
Written:
2003
Genre:
Dystopian, science fiction, adventure
Audience:
Middle grade / Young adult
Setting:
First book in the Books of Ember series (books total)
The story takes place in Ember, an underground city built centuries ago to shelter the last of humanity after a global disaster. The city's creators, known as the Builders, intended for people to live there for 200 years, after which instructions to return to the surface would be revealed. But over time, the instructions were lost, and now the city is in crisis.he power supply is failing, food is running out, and blackouts are becoming more frequent. The citizens live in fear but have no knowledge of any world beyond Ember.Two 12-year-olds, Lina Mayfleet and Doon Harrow, begin to suspect the truth about Ember's condition. Lina discovers a mysterious, partially destroyed document that appears to contain directions for escaping the city. Together, Lina and Doon work to decode it while dealing with corruption, fear, and disbelief from the adults around them.After solving the clues, they find a hidden path that leads them to the surface — a bright, natural world above ground. From a cliff, they throw down a message to help others in Ember find the way out.

fahrenheit
Author:
Ray Bradbury
Written:
9153
Genre:
Dystopian fiction, Science fiction
Title Meaning:
The temperature at which book paper catches fire and burns (451°F)
Fahrenheit 451 by Ray Bradbury is a dystopian novel set in a future society where books are banned and "firemen" burn any that are found. The story follows Guy Montag, a fireman who begins to question the purpose of his job and the oppressive, shallow society he lives in. Sparked by encounters with his curious young neighbor Clarisse and the traumatic suicide of a woman who chooses to die with her books, Montag starts secretly reading, seeking meaning and truth. As he grows more rebellious, he turns to a former English professor, Faber, for guidance. Montag eventually escapes the city and joins a group of intellectual drifters who have memorized entire books to preserve knowledge for a future society. The novel explores themes of censorship, conformity, the dehumanizing effects of technology, and the transformative power of literature and free thought. Bradbury’s powerful critique of a passive, media-obsessed world remains chillingly relevant today.

The Giver
Author:
Lois Lowry
Written:
1993
Genre:
Dystopian fiction, Science fiction, Young Adult.
Series:
First book in The Giver Quartet
The Giver by Lois Lowry is a dystopian novel set in a seemingly perfect society that has eliminated pain, conflict, and emotion by enforcing strict rules, sameness, and the erasure of personal memories. The story follows Jonas, a 12-year-old boy selected to become the Receiver of Memory, the only person who holds the truth about the world's past—memories of love, war, color, and suffering. As Jonas receives these memories from his mentor, The Giver, he begins to see the dark reality behind his community's controlled peace and lack of true freedom. Disturbed by what he learns—especially the community's practice of euthanasia—Jonas ultimately decides to risk everything to escape and bring change. The novel explores themes of individuality, memory, freedom, and the importance of emotional depth, making it a powerful and thought-provoking story that remains a staple in young adult literature.

HungerGames
Author:
Suzanne Collins.
Written:
2009.
Genre:
Dystopian, Young Adult, Science Fiction.
Series:
First book in The Hunger Games Trilogy (followed by Catching Fire and Mockingjay)
The Hunger Games is a dystopian novel by Suzanne Collins, published in 2008, and the first book in the bestselling trilogy (Catching Fire and Mockingjay follow). Set in the totalitarian nation of Panem, a future version of North America, the story follows Katniss Everdeen, a 16-year-old girl from the impoverished District 12. Each year, the Capitol forces one boy and one girl from each of the 12 districts to participate in the Hunger Games—a televised death match meant to both entertain and remind the districts of their subjugation after a failed rebellion. When Katniss volunteers to take her younger sister's place, she is thrown into a brutal fight for survival against 23 other tributes, including Peeta Mellark, a boy from her district who may or may not be her ally. As the Games unfold, Katniss must navigate deadly threats, manipulate public perception, and challenge the cruelty of the Capitol. The novel explores themes of oppression, survival, media manipulation, inequality, rebellion, and sacrifice, with a strong, morally complex heroine at its center. It has received widespread acclaim for its intense plot, sharp social commentary, and emotional depth, and was adapted into a successful film series starring Jennifer Lawrence.

Maze Runner
Author:
James Dashner
Written:
2009
Genre:
Young Adult, Science Fiction, Dystopian.
Series:
First book in The Maze Runner series (followed by The Scorch Trials, The Death Cure, etc.)
The Maze Runner by James Dashner is a thrilling young adult dystopian novel centered on Thomas,a teenage boy who suddenly finds himself trapped in the Glade—a large, enclosed area surrounded by towerin stone walls that open to a massive and ever-shifting Maze. Thomas arrives with no memory of who he is or how he got there, just like the other boys living in the Glade, who have established their own society governed by rules and assigned roles to survive. The Maze itself is a deadly puzzle filled with lethal creatures called Grievers, and for years the boys, known as Gladers, have tried unsuccessfully to solve it and find an escape. Thomas's arrival triggers a series of events, including the appearance of the first girl, Teresa, who shares a mysterious telepathic connection with him. As Thomas learns more about the Maze, his unique instincts and bravery push him to challenge the established order, inspiring the Gladers to band together to unravel the secrets of their captivity. The novel explores deep themes such as the importance of teamwork and leadership, the search for identity amidst lost memories, and the consequences of manipulation and control by unseen forces. Full of suspense, action, and mystery, The Maze Runner is the start of a larger story about survival and rebellion against a dystopian experiment that tests human endurance and spirit.

Mind Games by Kiersten White is a gripping young adult paranormal thriller centered on Ruby “Riv” Kennedy, a strong and determined teenage girl born with extraordinary psychic abilities, including telekinesis and mind-reading. Raised and trained by her father to combat a secret and sinister organization called the Agents—who exploit people with similar powers for their own dark purposes—Riv's life is thrown into chaos when her father is captured by the very enemies they've been fighting against. Forced to infiltrate the Agents undercover, Riv navigates a dangerous world of deception, shifting alliances, and hidden agendas, all while grappling with her own sense of identity and morality. As she uncovers the deeper truths behind the ongoing conflict, Riv must decide whom to trust and how far she's willing to go to save her family and protect others like her. With a fast-paced narrative full of supernatural action, psychological intrigue, and emotional depth, Mind Games explores complex themes of power, control, loyalty, and the often-blurred line between good and evil, making it a compelling read for fans of paranormal suspense and YA mysteries.

Lord Of The Files
Author:
William Golding.
Written:
1954
Genre:
Allegorical novel, Dystopian fiction, Psychological fiction.
Lord of the Flies by William Golding is a powerful allegorical novel about a group of British boys stranded on a deserted island without adult supervision. As they try to govern themselves and await rescue, the boys fragile sense of civilization quickly breaks down, leading to chaos, violence, and savagery. Through characters like Ralph, who represents order, and Jack, who embodies primal instincts and the lust for power, the story explores deep themes such as the conflict between civilization and barbarism, the loss of innocence, and the inherent darkness within human nature. Symbolism plays a key role, with objects like the conch shell symbolizing authority and the "Lord of the Flies" (a pig's head on a stick) representing the evil lurking inside everyone. Golding's novel serves as a haunting commentary on society, human behavior, and the thin line separating order from chaos.
